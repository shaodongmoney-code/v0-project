Every employer in the United States must verify the identity and work
authorization of each new hire using **Form I-9**. It sounds simple, but I-9
mistakes are among the most frequent — and most expensive — compliance errors,
especially for companies new to the U.S. market.

## Why I-9 matters

The form exists to confirm that every employee is legally authorized to work in
the United States. Penalties apply not only for hiring unauthorized workers, but
also for paperwork errors alone. Fines accumulate per form, so small process gaps
can become significant liabilities.

## The checklist

### Section 1 — Employee

- Must be completed by the employee no later than their **first day** of work.
- Verify that all required fields are filled in and the form is signed and dated.

### Section 2 — Employer

- Must be completed within **three business days** of the start date.
- Physically examine original documents that establish identity and work
  authorization.
- Record document titles, numbers, and expiration dates accurately.

### Ongoing

1. Store I-9 forms separately from personnel files.
2. Track work authorization expiration dates and re-verify on time.
3. Retain forms for the required period: three years after hire or one year after
   termination, whichever is later.

> Do not ask for specific documents. The employee chooses which acceptable
> documents to present from the official lists. Demanding particular documents can
> itself be a violation.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- Completing Section 2 late or not at all
- Accepting expired documents
- Missing signatures or dates
- Failing to re-verify expiring authorization
- Storing I-9s where they are hard to retrieve during an audit

## Build the habit early

The easiest way to stay compliant is to make I-9 verification a fixed step in your
onboarding workflow, with a clear owner and a calendar reminder for re-verification
dates. A few minutes of discipline on day one prevents costly surprises later.
