When companies expand into the United States, one of the first surprises is how
different employment documentation looks compared to most other countries. There
is no single "employment contract" that covers everything. Instead, the
relationship is shaped by a combination of an offer letter, company policies, and
a handful of targeted agreements.

## At-will employment is the default

In nearly every U.S. state, employment is **at-will**. This means either the
employer or the employee can end the relationship at any time, for any lawful
reason, with or without notice. Because of this, U.S. offer letters are usually
short and explicitly state that employment is at-will — rather than promising a
fixed term.

This is very different from jurisdictions where fixed-term contracts and
statutory notice periods are the norm. Trying to import a European-style contract
into the U.S. often creates confusion and can unintentionally undermine at-will
status.

## The offer letter

The offer letter is the cornerstone document. A well-drafted offer letter
typically covers:

- Job title and a brief description of the role
- Start date and work location
- Compensation, including base salary and any bonus structure
- Exempt or non-exempt classification under the FLSA
- A clear at-will employment statement
- Any contingencies, such as background checks or I-9 verification

## Agreements that actually matter

Beyond the offer letter, a few agreements carry real legal weight:

1. **Confidentiality / NDA** — protects trade secrets and sensitive information.
2. **Invention assignment** — ensures work product belongs to the company.
3. **Restrictive covenants** — non-solicitation and, where enforceable, non-compete
   clauses. Enforceability varies dramatically by state.

> A clause that is standard and enforceable in one state may be void in another.
> California, for example, broadly prohibits non-compete agreements.

## Practical guidance

Keep the offer letter clean and at-will, and move detailed obligations into
separate signed agreements. This structure is easier to maintain, easier to
update, and far more defensible if a dispute ever arises. When in doubt, align the
documents with the specific state where the employee will work — not where your
headquarters happens to be.
