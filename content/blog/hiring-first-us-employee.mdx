Making your first hire in the United States is an exciting milestone — and a moment
where getting the foundations right pays off for years. Here is a practical path
from "we want to hire someone" to a fully onboarded, compliant employee.

## 1. Establish a legal basis to employ

Before you can put someone on payroll, you generally need:

- A U.S. legal entity (or a registered Employer of Record arrangement)
- A **Federal Employer Identification Number (EIN)**
- State tax and unemployment registrations in the state where the employee works

If standing up an entity is premature, an Employer of Record (EOR) lets you hire
compliantly while you validate the market.

## 2. Classify the role correctly

Decide two things early:

1. **Employee vs. contractor** — misclassification is a major risk area.
2. **Exempt vs. non-exempt** under the FLSA, which determines overtime eligibility.

Getting these wrong is one of the most common and costly mistakes for new
employers.

## 3. Set compensation and benefits

Research market pay for the role and location. Decide on base salary, any bonus,
and the benefits you will offer — health insurance, PTO, and a retirement plan
such as a 401(k). Even a lean first offer should be competitive for the local
market.

## 4. Prepare the paperwork

- A clean, at-will offer letter
- Confidentiality and invention assignment agreements
- Form I-9 and Form W-4
- State-specific new hire reporting

## 5. Run onboarding intentionally

A structured first 30–60–90 days helps a remote U.S. hire feel connected to a team
that may be thousands of miles away. Set clear goals, schedule regular check-ins,
and document the basics so nothing depends on a single conversation.

> Your first hire often becomes the cultural anchor for your entire U.S. presence.
> Invest in the experience accordingly.

## The payoff

Foreign companies that treat the first hire as an infrastructure project — not just
a recruiting task — build a base that scales smoothly. Get the entity,
classification, and onboarding right, and every subsequent hire becomes easier.
